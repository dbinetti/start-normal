The Four Pillars Can't Sustain Weight

All told, there are four main components to the framework, marketed as the “Four Pillars”: Hygiene, Masks, Distance and Gatherings.

Hygenie is mostly about washing hands and cleaning facilities and aside from some irritated skin from too much hand sanitizer is largely a reasonable assumption. They mandate daily temperature checks on children which is likely to create some privacy concerns, but hygiene is at least doable.

Masks are more problematic. They are expected to be worn at all times by all staff, visitors, and students from K through 12. The framework cites an extensive review by an international consortium of scientists which found that well-fitting masks reduce transmissibility, which is true. But the key assumption is “well-fitting”, which means that the wearers have to put them on properly and keep them on at all times. This is a plausible assumption for high school, and perhaps upper middle. But at elementary school and below this is wholly unrealistic. And how do you teach phonics wearing a mask? Will parents accept illiteracy as the price of "safety"?

Distance is the requirement that students and staff stay six feet apart at all times, and is far and away the most unrealistic assumption. Due to classroom size constraints, the framework mandates a staggered schedule where half the students receive in-person instruction and half distance-learning, but that assumes teachers do both in-person and distance learning simultaneously. Now our teachers are miracle workers in many ways, but doing two jobs at the same time is a mathematical impossibility — and that is before the budget cuts. I could continue to provide dozens of examples where distance assumption fails against reality, but the fact the framework doesn’t address this very obvious issue with teachers is a little troubling.

Gatherings prohibits all ‘non-required’ activities, including drama, music, sports, assemblies, libraries, cafeterias and physical education (aside from solitary activities). This assumes that these are ‘non-required’, with which many parents and child development professionals disagree.

In summary, three of these four pillars are flimsily constructed and won’t hold our children’s weight. And when all this comes crashing down everyone will suffer.
